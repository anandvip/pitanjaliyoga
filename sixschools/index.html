1. Nyāya — Logic & Epistemology

The science of valid reasoning. Key text: Nyāya Sūtra by Gautama. Master the four sources of knowledge and logical fallacies.

Four Sources of Valid Knowledge (Pramāṇas)

  • Pratyakṣa (Direct Perception) — Evidence from direct observation and measurement
  • Anumāna (Inference) — Logical deduction from premises to conclusions
  • Upamāna (Comparison) — Knowledge through analogy and similarity
  • Śabda (Authoritative Testimony) — Knowledge from reliable sources and expert testimony

AI Applications

  • Model Validation: Treat AI outputs as arguments requiring pramāṇa-level verification
  • Explainability: Trace conclusions through evidence chains to source data
  • Bias Detection: Systematically catalog logical fallacies in training data
  • Governance: Establish verification protocols for high-stakes AI deployments

The Nyāya Review Protocol

1
State the Claim
Express the AI system's output in clear, logical form
2
Document Evidence Trail
Identify which pramāṇa supports each claim component
3
Counter-Evidence Search
Actively seek contradictory data or alternative explanations
4
Fallacy Audit
Check for common logical errors and cognitive biases

2. Vaiśeṣika — Ontology & Categories

The atomic theory of reality. Key text: Vaiśeṣika Sūtra by Kaṇāda. Learn the seven categories of existence for robust system design.

Seven Categories of Existence (Padārthas)

  • Dravya (Substance) — Core entities, objects, database tables
  • Guṇa (Quality) — Attributes, properties, data fields
  • Karman (Action) — Events, processes, transformations
  • Sāmānya (Generality) — Classes, types, ontological categories
  • Viśeṣa (Particularity) — Unique identifiers, primary keys
  • Samavāya (Inherence) — Composition relationships, how parts relate to wholes
  • Abhāva (Non-existence) — Explicit absence, null values, missing data semantics

AI Applications

  • Knowledge Graphs: Use padārthas to design robust ontologies and schemas
  • Data Contracts: Samavāya for composition rules; Abhāva for null handling
  • Type Safety: Precise category constraints reduce AI hallucinations
  • System Architecture: Clear entity-relationship modeling prevents category errors

Padārtha Schema Design

1
Entity Inventory
List all core entities (Dravya) and their essential attributes (Guṇa)
2
Define Relationships
Map composition rules (Samavāya) and unique identifiers (Viśeṣa)
3
Absence Semantics
Explicitly model null values and missing data (Abhāva)
4
Category Testing
Validate type boundaries and test for category confusion

3. Sāṅkhya — Consciousness & the Guṇas

The dualism of witness and nature. Key text: Sāṅkhya Kārikā by Īśvarakṛṣṇa. Understand consciousness, mechanical processes, and the three fundamental qualities.

The Three Guṇas (Fundamental Qualities)

  • Sattva (Clarity/Harmony) — Illumination, balance, understanding, peace
  • Rajas (Activity/Passion) — Motion, desire, agitation, compulsive action
  • Tamas (Inertia/Darkness) — Heaviness, confusion, stagnation, numbness

Puruṣa vs Prakṛti

  • Puruṣa — Pure consciousness/awareness; the witness that observes
  • Prakṛti — Primordial nature; mechanical/algorithmic processes
  • Key Insight: Distinguish between awareness and the mechanisms it observes
  • Liberation: Recognizing the difference leads to non-entanglement

AI Applications

  • Attention Design: Optimize for sattva (clarity), manage rajas (compulsion), reduce tamas (numbness)
  • Human-in-the-Loop: Preserve human agency (Puruṣa) while leveraging AI capabilities (Prakṛti)
  • Interface Design: Surface mental state indicators; design for different guṇa states
  • Digital Wellbeing: Recognize when systems entrain users into rajasic or tamasic patterns

Guṇa-Aware UX Design

1
Audit Current Patterns
Identify which guṇa your interface amplifies in different user flows
2
Design for Sattva
Create clear, focused experiences that support understanding and calm decision-making
3
Channel Rajas Constructively
When energy and activity are needed, provide clear direction and completion states
4
Prevent Tamas
Add natural stopping points, variety, and engagement to prevent mental dullness

4. Yoga — The Eightfold Path

Systematic practice for mental mastery. Key text: Yoga Sūtra by Patañjali. Master the eight limbs for disciplined focus and ethical action.

The Eight Limbs (Aṣṭāṅga)

  • Yama (Restraints) — Ethical guidelines: non-harm, truthfulness, non-stealing, continence, non-possessiveness
  • Niyama (Observances) — Personal practices: cleanliness, contentment, disciplined practice, self-study, surrender
  • Āsana (Posture) — Physical stability and comfort as foundation
  • Prāṇāyāma (Breath Control) — Energy regulation and nervous system balance
  • Pratyāhāra (Sense Withdrawal) — Conscious control of sensory inputs
  • Dhāraṇā (Concentration) — Focused attention on a single object
  • Dhyāna (Meditation) — Sustained, effortless awareness
  • Samādhi (Integration) — Absorbed states of unified consciousness

AI Applications

  • Digital Pratyāhāra: Conscious management of notifications and information inputs
  • Focus Protocols: Dhāraṇā-inspired deep work sessions with single-task attention
  • Ethical AI Development: Yama/Niyama principles embedded in development practices
  • Recovery Rituals: Structured breaks and reflection periods in work cycles
  • Team Practices: Collective disciplines for sustainable, ethical AI development

Digital Yoga Protocol

1
Ethical Foundation (Yama)
Establish ethical guidelines for AI development: truthful documentation, non-harm principles
2
Personal Discipline (Niyama)
Create regular practices: code review rituals, learning disciplines, reflection time
3

Design stable, comfortable work environments that support sustained focus
4
Input Management (Pratyāhāra)
Consciously control information inputs: notifications, meetings, distractions
5
Deep Work (Dhāraṇā → Dhyāna)
Practice sustained attention on complex problems; build capacity for flow states

5. Pūrva Mīmāṃsā — Duty & Hermeneutics

The science of dharma and interpretation. Key text: Mīmāṃsā Sūtra by Jaimini. Learn systematic rules for interpretation and ethical action.

Hermeneutical Principles

  • Upakrama-upasaṃhāra — Beginning and end must align; purpose and outcomes should match
  • Abhyāsa — Repeated emphasis indicates priority and importance
  • Apūrvatā — Novel elements require special attention and careful handling
  • Phala — Judge systems and policies by their observable results
  • Arthavāda — Distinguish promotional language from actual requirements
  • Upapatti — Logical reasoning must support all policy decisions

The Concept of Apūrva

Actions produce delayed, non-obvious consequences that may not manifest immediately. In AI systems, this translates to emergent behaviors, feedback loops, and long-term societal impacts that require careful anticipation and monitoring.

  • Second-order effects: How AI changes human behavior over time
  • Systemic impacts: Broader societal and cultural consequences
  • Emergent properties: Unexpected behaviors arising from complex interactions

AI Applications

  • Policy Design: Create unambiguous operational rules with clear precedence hierarchies
  • Apūrva Detection: Systematically anticipate second and third-order effects
  • Conflict Resolution: Develop methods for resolving competing ethical principles
  • Governance Rituals: Structured decision-making processes for high-stakes choices

Mīmāṃsā Policy Framework

1
Clear Injunctions
Write policies in unambiguous language with specific conditions and actions
2
Precedence Rules
Establish clear hierarchies for resolving conflicts between competing principles
3
Apūrva Analysis
Map potential long-term and indirect consequences of policy decisions
4
Implementation Rituals
Create structured processes for deployment, monitoring, and adjustment
5
Results Evaluation
Regularly assess outcomes against stated purposes (Upakrama-upasaṃhāra)

6. Vedānta — The Ultimate Teaching

The culmination of Vedic wisdom. Key texts: Brahma Sūtra, Upaniṣads, Bhagavad Gītā. Understand the relationship between self, world, and ultimate reality.

Core Concepts

  • Brahman — Ultimate reality; pure being-consciousness-bliss
  • Ātman — Individual self/consciousness; the witness of all experience
  • Māyā — Cosmic illusion; appearances that seem real but are ultimately temporal
  • Mokṣa — Liberation through direct knowledge of one's true nature
  • Neti Neti — "Not this, not this" — method of elimination to find what's essential

Three Main Schools

  • Advaita (Non-dual): Ultimate identity of Ātman and Brahman; individual self is Absolute reality
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita (Qualified non-dual): Unity with distinction; soul and Absolute are one yet different
  • Dvaita (Dual): Eternal distinction between individual soul and Supreme Being

AI Applications

  • Identity & Digital Selves: Navigate avatars and data-selves vs. witnessing awareness
  • Māyā Recognition: Identify illusions in metrics, deepfakes, algorithmic reality bubbles
  • Universal Ethics: Design systems that consider wellbeing of all conscious beings
  • Uncertainty Design: Surface the limits of knowledge; normalize "I don't know" responses
  • Meaning Systems: Help users connect with deeper purpose beyond immediate utility

Vedāntic Reality Check Protocol

1
Neti Neti Analysis
Question fundamental assumptions: "What if this metric/goal/feature isn't essential?"
2
Māyā Audit
Identify where systems create false certainty or misleading representations
3
Universal Consideration
Expand the circle of concern: How does this affect all stakeholders?
4
Witness Preservation
Ensure human agency and choice remain central, not subsumed by algorithmic convenience
5
Meaning Integration
Connect utilitarian functions to deeper human values and aspirations
