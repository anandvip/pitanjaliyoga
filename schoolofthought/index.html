1) Nyāya — Logic & Epistemology

#

Key text: Nyāya Sūtra (Gautama). Core: valid knowledge (pramāṇa), debate, and fallacies.

Essence

  • Pramāṇas: perception (pratyakṣa), inference (anumāna), comparison (upamāna), testimony (śabda).
  • Logical method: careful definitions, valid inference, exposure of fallacies (hetvābhāsa).
  • Telos: reliable cognition to remove suffering through right knowledge.
Typical fallacies to watch for (modernized)
  • Asiddha (unproven ground): model claim without verified data lineage.
  • Savyabhicāra (inconstant): correlation presented as causal across changing contexts.
  • Viruddha (contradictory): conclusion clashes with known constraints/specs.
  • Satpratipakṣa (counterbalanced): strong opposing evidence ignored/omitted.
  • Bādhita (defeated): observation overturns theory, yet claim persists.

AI‑Age Relevance

  • Model claims as arguments: treat outputs as propositions requiring pramāṇa‑level support.
  • Explainability: trace from conclusion → evidence → data lineage → authority (śabda).
  • Debiasing: catalogue and test for hetvābhāsas in training narratives and prompts.
  • Governance: adopt a public Nyāya docket for major AI deployments.

What Nyāya would ask now

  • Which pramāṇa grounds this model’s recommendation in this context?
  • Is there contradictory observation in the wild that bādhita this claim?
  • Have we provided śabda (qualified testimony) with audit‑ready citations?

Checklist

  • Document pramāṇa per feature: P (sensing), A (inference), U (analogy), Ś (authority).
  • Maintain a fallacy register observed during evals.
  • Require counter‑evidence searches before policy adoption.

Prompts for inquiry

“List the pramāṇa justifying X.” “What evidence could bādhita this?” “Show logical form of the claim.”

2) Vaiśeṣika — Ontology & Categories

#

Key text: Vaiśeṣika Sūtra (Kaṇāda). Core: categories (padārtha), atomism, realism.

Essence

  • Padārthas: substance (dravya), quality (guṇa), action (karman), generality (sāmānya), particularity (viśeṣa), inherence (samavāya), non‑existence (abhāva).
  • Focus: what exists and how parts bind to wholes.

AI‑Age Relevance

  • Knowledge graphs & schemas: use padārthas to design ontologies and types.
  • Data contracts: samavāya for composition; abhāva for missingness/nulls.
  • Safety: precise type constraints reduce category errors and hallucinations.
Padārtha → Data/AI design (quick mapping)
  • Dravya: entities/tables/objects; Guṇa: fields/attributes; Karman: events/transforms.
  • Sāmānya: class/ontology; Viśeṣa: identifiers; Samavāya: composition/foreign keys.
  • Abhāva: explicit null semantics; absence as first‑class fact.

What Vaiśeṣika would ask now

  • What exactly are the entities and their essential qualities?
  • How do wholes inherit properties from parts without confusion?
  • Have we modeled absence and negation explicitly?

Checklist

  • Define ontology before training; pin IDs (viśeṣa).
  • Write null/unknown policies (abhāva), not just defaults.
  • Document composition edges (samavāya) and invariants.

Prompts

“Schema as padārthas.” “List invariants for composition.” “Negation/absence rules.”

3) Sāṅkhya — Purusha/Prakriti & the Guṇas

#

Key text: Sāṅkhya Kārikā (Īśvarakṛṣṇa). Core: 25 tattvas; dualism of witness (puruṣa) and nature (prakṛti); guṇas (sattva/rajas/tamas).

Essence

  • Witness vs. mechanism: distinguish awareness from processes.
  • Guṇas: clarity/harmony (sattva), activity/desire (rajas), inertia/dullness (tamas).
  • Goal: discernment (viveka) leading to non‑entanglement.

AI‑Age Relevance

  • Attention ecology: design for sattva (clarity), manage rajas (compulsion), reduce tamas (numb scroll).
  • Human‑in‑the‑loop: keep the witness sovereign; avoid conflating users with metrics.
  • Psychotech hygiene: interfaces that surface inner state (e.g., satiation/overload meters).
Micro‑practices (90‑second resets)
  • Sattva boost: 4‑7‑8 breaths; write 1 line intention; set 25‑min focus timer.
  • Rajas channel: convert urge → task list; move body for 60s.
  • Tamas lift: cold water splash; bright light; posture shift.

What Sāṅkhya would ask now

  • Are we confusing algorithmic behavior with the nature of awareness?
  • Does the product entrain rajas/tamas? Where can we deliberately seed sattva?
  • What is the minimal entanglement to accomplish the user’s end?

Checklist

  • Ship focus modes by default; visible end‑states to avoid endless loops.
  • Provide friction to impulsive actions; ease for deliberate ones.
  • Track overload indicators and suggest recovery.

Prompts

“Name the guna this flow amplifies.” “Where is the witness forgotten?” “Design for non‑entanglement.”

4) Yoga — Practice & Liberation

#

Key text: Yoga Sūtra (Patañjali). Core: eight limbs; kleshas; disciplined practice.

Essence

  • Aṣṭāṅga: yama, niyama, āsana, prāṇāyāma, pratyāhāra, dhāraṇā, dhyāna, samādhi.
  • Kleśas: ignorance, ego‑fixation, attachment, aversion, clinging to life.
  • Method: repeatable protocols to pacify fluctuations of mind (citta‑vṛtti‑nirodha).

AI‑Age Relevance

  • Digital wellbeing: pratyāhāra = control inputs; set boundaries on notifications/feeds.
  • Peak work: dhāraṇā→dhyāna = deep work blocks, monotask; post‑block recovery.
  • Team hygiene: yama/niyama ethics for builders (truthful docs, non‑harm, austerity).
Protocol: 25‑minute Focus Sprint (Yoga‑inspired)
  1. 2 min breath + intention (niyama).
  2. 25 min monotask (dhāraṇā→dhyāna).
  3. 3 min eyes‑closed rest (pratyāhāra).
  4. Review: note one learning, one obstacle (svādhyāya).

What Yoga would ask now

  • Does this tool reduce mental scattering or amplify it?
  • Where is non‑harm (ahiṃsā) violated—users, workers, environment?
  • What daily practice integrates the insight, not just reads about it?

Checklist

  • Ship Do‑Not‑Disturb presets; 60–90 min deep work scenes.
  • Ethics rubric embedded into PR templates (yama/niyama prompts).
  • Recovery rituals post‑launch; retros with compassion and truthfulness.

Prompts

“Design a pratyāhāra mode for X.” “Name the klesha[such as anxiety, fear, anger, jealousy, desire, etc] at play.” “From vṛtti[streams of consciousness] → nirodha[cessation] steps?”

5) Pūrva Mīmāṃsā — Duty, Ritual & Hermeneutics

#

Key text: Mīmāṃsā Sūtra (Jaimini). Core: dharma from scriptural injunction; interpretive rules; apurva (unseen efficacy).

Essence

  • Normativity: how we know what we ought to do.
  • Exegesis: rules to resolve conflicts, context, general vs. specific, etc.
  • Apurva: actions produce delayed, non‑obvious consequences.

AI‑Age Relevance

  • Policy & alignment: write unambiguous operational rules; resolve collisions.
  • Second‑order effects: anticipate apurva—long‑tail harms, feedback loops.
  • Reproducibility: ritualized runbooks for safety‑critical changes.
Six interpretive heuristics → policy design
  • Upakrama‑upasaṃhāra (beginning‑end): align purpose and outcomes.
  • Abhyāsa (repetition): repeated emphasis → priority in policy.
  • Apūrvatā (novelty): flag novel risks for special scrutiny.
  • Phala (result): judge by consequences measured.
  • Arthavāda (commendation): separate hype from injunction.
  • Upapatti (reasoning): require rationale trace for each rule.

What Mīmāṃsā would ask now

  • What exactly is enjoined, by whom, to whom, in which context?
  • Which rules override in conflict, and why?
  • What apurva might emerge months later?

Checklist

  • Policy specs with precedence tables.
  • Change rituals: pre‑mortems, staged rollouts, canaries.
  • Measure lagging outcomes and feedback to rules.

Prompts

“Write the injunction unambiguously.” “Resolve this rule collision.” “List plausible apurva effects.”

6) Vedānta — Brahman/Ātman & Ultimate Meaning

#

Key text: Brahma Sūtra (Bādarāyaṇa) + Upaniṣads + Gītā. Major views: Advaita (non‑dual), Viśiṣṭādvaita (qualified non‑dual), Dvaita (dual).

Essence

  • Self & Absolute: reality of Brahman; relation to self and world varies by school.
  • Māyā & discernment: appearance vs. reality; inquiry (neti neti).
  • Telos: liberation through knowledge/devotion/service (per sub‑school).

AI‑Age Relevance

  • Identity in the digital: avatars, data‑selves vs. witnessing awareness.
  • Illusion management: deepfakes/metrics as māyā; train skepticism.
  • Universal regard: non‑dual ethic → widen circle of concern.
Sub‑schools, one‑line relevance
  • Advaita: beware reifying narratives; practice de‑identification with dashboards.
  • Viśiṣṭādvaita: relational self → design for devotion/service/embeddedness.
  • Dvaita: clear distinction → accountability, moral agency preserved.

What Vedānta would ask now

  • Who is the user beyond roles and analytics shadows?
  • What is real benefit vs. metric illusion?
  • How does this system cultivate compassion and clarity?

Checklist

  • Surface uncertainty; avoid false precision.
  • Expose model limits; normalize “I don’t know.”
  • Add reflection prompts at key decision points.

Prompts

“What here is māyā vs. real?” “Run a neti‑neti audit.” “Design for universal regard.”

Practical Kits

#

Nyāya Docket

  1. State the claim in standard logical form.
  2. Attach pramāṇa trail (P/A/U/Ś) with links to evidence.
  3. List counter‑evidence searched + results.
  4. Record fallacies detected and mitigations.

Padārtha Schema Audit

  1. Entity/attribute/event inventory.
  2. Composition invariants and IDs.
  3. Null/absence semantics written.
  4. Type boundary tests (fuzz for category errors).

Guna‑Aware UX

  1. Mark flows that stoke rajas/tamas.
  2. Default to focus‑first layouts.
  3. Offer deliberate pacing/stop‑rules.
  4. Reflect state & suggest resets.

Yoga Focus Protocol

  1. Monotask schedule + pratyāhāra guardrails.
  2. Breathing + micro‑recovery between sprints.
  3. Ethics rubric on PRs & product docs.
  4. Team retrospective cadence.

Mīmāṃsā Policy Table

  1. Rule inventory with precedence tiers.
  2. Change‑rituals & sign‑offs.
  3. Lagging indicators to monitor.
  4. Apurva catalogue: hypothesize, test, adapt.

Vedānta Maya Checks

  1. Illusion audit (deepfakes, vanity metrics).
  2. Uncertainty surfacing + limits messaging.
  3. Compassion prompts in decision flows.
  4. Self/role separation reminders.

Canonical Sources & Study Tips

#

For primary study, look for reliable translations/commentaries of: Nyāya Sūtra, Vaiśeṣika Sūtra, Sāṅkhya Kārikā, Yoga Sūtra with Vyāsa Bhāṣya, Mīmāṃsā Sūtra, and the Upaniṣads/Bhagavad‑Gītā/Brahma Sūtra for Vedānta. Pair with a modern secondary source that distinguishes traditions clearly.

  • Study one school per week; keep a Nyāya‑style docket of your learnings.
  • Practice something (Yoga) while you read; design something (Vaiśeṣika) while you model; question something (Vedānta) while you ship.
